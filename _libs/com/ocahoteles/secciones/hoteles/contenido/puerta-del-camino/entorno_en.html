Santiago de Compostela

The city of Santiago de Compostela (Galicia) is the well-known end of the Camino de Santiago, where thousands of pilgrims from all over the world arrive every year. This city was declared UNESCO World Heritage in 1985 and it is nowadays the capital of the Autonomous Community of Galicia. Santiago has a lot to offer to tourists, especially its old quarter:


• Obradoiro Square: surrounded by buildings with high architectonic value, the vast Obradoiro Square is named after bakery workshop that was in the square during the construction of the Cathedral. 

• The Cathedral of Santiago: it is the end of the Camino, the Cathedral is the main building of the square and of the city of Santiago de Compostela. The beginning of the construction goes back to the year 1075 and it was decorated and extended during the XVI and XVIII centuries. It was declared Property of Cultural Interest in 1896 and it is worth to visit:


• The roof of the Cathedral

• The museum of the Cathedral

• Pórtico de La Gloria

• Crypt

• Botafumerio exhibition 


• Pazo de Xelmírez: it is the building attached to the Cathedral, very important in the civil architecture, and built between 1120 and 1136. 

• Hostal de los Reyes Católicos: it was built because of the visit of the Catholic Monarchs in 1486 and at the beginning it was an hospice and hospital so pilgrims could rest at the end of the trail. Nowadays, it is still used as a hotel.

• Pazo de Raxoi: it is a building opposite the Cathedral and it was built in the XVIII century under the command of the Archbishop Bartolomé de Rajoy. Nowadays it belongs to the city hall of Santiago de Compostela and the Xunta of Galicia.

• San Jerónimo School: founded by the Archbishop Alonso III de Fonseca in the XVI century for poor students. Currently, it is where the vice-chancellor’s office of the University of Santiago is located nowadays. 


In the charming old quarter of Santiago de Compostela, we recommend you to visit the following places:


• La Quintana Square: located by the rear façade of the Cathedral, through this square you can directly enter to the puerta Santa. The square is also called “Square of the literaries” after the Literary Battalion, a group of students who fought against Napoleon. 

• Platerías Square: located on a side of the Cathedral. At the centre you can admire the Baroque Fuente de los caballos. 

• Casa del Cabildo: this building can be found in the Platerías Square and it is a Baroque building from the XVIII century, built by the architect Clemente Fernández Sarela.

• Monastery of San Martín Pinario: originally a Benedictine monastery from the XI century, it is the second biggest monastery in Spain, after San Lorenzo del Escorial. 

• Rúa do Vilar: at one side of the street you can see connected porticos but, on the other side of the streets, the porticos are separated. 

• Rúa Nova: on this street we must point out the Santa María Church, the Teatro Principal, the Pazo de Pedrosa and the Balconada House.

• Do Toural Square: where you can find the Pazo de Bendaña and where the Rúa Nova and Rúa do Villar meet. 

• Chapel of Ánimas: located on the Rúa Casas Reais at the entrance of the French Way in the old quarter. The chapel was built between 1784 and 1788 by the Architect Miguel Ferro Caaveiro.

• Mazarelos Square and Mazarelos Arch: also called “University Square” it has the only remaining arch of the wall that surrounded the city of Santiago de Compostela.
